Why Ignoring Risk in Capital Budgeting Might Be a Company's Biggest Mistake

By Manuel Bermúdez & Santiago Londoño

Every business wants to grow, innovate, and stay ahead of the competition. But behind every bold move—whether it’s launching a new product or opening a new plant—there’s one crucial question:

Are we accounting for the risk involved?

📊 Risk Is Not the Enemy—Blindness Is

Capital budgeting involves committing large sums of money to long-term projects. That’s not something to take lightly. When executives ignore risk, they assume the future will play out as expected. But let’s face it—things rarely go as planned.

By using tools like risk-adjusted discount rates, scenario analysis, or sensitivity analysis, managers can visualize a range of outcomes—not just the “best case.” This helps them avoid overestimating returns and underestimating uncertainty.

“Risk doesn’t disappear by ignoring it. It gets more expensive later.” —Adapted from Damodaran (2012)

💸 What Happens When Risk Is Ignored?

  • Overconfident decisions lead to overinvestment.
  • Projects get approved based on inflated NPVs.
  • The company becomes vulnerable to cost overruns or delays.
  • Future borrowing costs go up due to loss of investor confidence.

🧐 Risk and Strategy Go Hand in Hand

Risk is not just a finance issue—it’s a strategic one. It tells you where to step carefully and where to double down. Ignoring risk is like playing chess with only half the board visible.

The use of CAPM and beta helps companies estimate the required return based on volatility, and tools like NPV and IRR guide them toward sound investments.

📌 Final Thoughts: Embrace the Uncertainty

Ignoring risk won’t make it disappear. In fact, it makes it more dangerous. Capital budgeting should always include a deep understanding of the uncertainty involved. Companies that build risk into their decision-making processes are more resilient, strategic, and profitable over time.

So, next time your company evaluates a major investment, ask yourself:

Are we looking at the numbers—or the full picture?
